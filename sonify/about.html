How it works

Turning the multi-probe microwave sky from the HalfDome simulations into sound — what the pieces are, and how each one is mapped to what you hear.

The idea

In cosmology the angular power spectrum — how much structure the sky has at each angular scale, indexed by multipole ℓ — is the central summary statistic. The analogy to audio is exact: the power spectrum of a sound is what gives it its timbre, so when we map a cosmological power spectrum onto an audible spectrum we are making the same mathematical object perceptible to a different sense. Big angular scales (small ℓ) become low pitches; fine scales (large ℓ) become high pitches.

The data: components & bands

Everything is built from the HalfDome simulation suite (spherical-harmonic maps to ℓmax=9000, smoothed with a 2′ beam). There are five things you can listen to or view:

  • CMB lensed cosmic microwave background — dominates the large scales (bass).
  • tSZ thermal Sunyaev–Zel'dovich effect from hot cluster gas — midrange, peaks near ℓ∼2000.
  • CIB cosmic infrared background from dusty galaxies — treble, dominates the small scales and the high frequencies.
  • kSZ kinetic Sunyaev–Zel'dovich effect — a faint Doppler signal.
  • Total the sum CMB + tSZ + CIB.

Six observing bands are grouped by survey: SO MF (93, 145 GHz), SO HF (225, 280 GHz), and Planck (143, 353 GHz). A key physical point: only tSZ and CIB depend on frequency. The CMB and kSZ are frequency-independent in thermodynamic units, so switching bands leaves them (and their sky patterns) unchanged — only the tSZ/CIB/Total mix moves. The tSZ in particular nearly vanishes near its ~217 GHz null (quietest at 225 GHz), while the CIB climbs steeply toward 353 GHz.

The ℓ → sound mapping

Multipole ℓ maps to audio frequency by a log–log scaling (ℓ=2 → 40 Hz, ℓ=6000 → 8 kHz), which respects human pitch perception. Under this mapping the CMB first acoustic peak (ℓ∼220) lands near middle C (~270 Hz), the tSZ peak (ℓ∼2000) at ~2.7 kHz, and the CIB peak (ℓ∼5000) at ~6.8 kHz. Amplitudes are globally normalized (against a fixed reference, not per-buffer) so that genuine variations in power are preserved — essential during sweeps, where loudness should track the power at the current scale.

Synthesis methods (Sonifier)

Shaped Noise filters white noise so its spectrum matches Dℓ — the most faithful representation; sounds textural. Additive sums sine tones weighted by √Dℓ — more tonal, with individual peaks audible as distinct pitches. Scale Layers splits the spectrum into a bass drone (ℓ<100), harmonic tones at the CMB acoustic peaks (ℓ=220, 540, 810), and high-ℓ bandpass noise — making the scale structure explicit as three timbres. Instruments plays the spectrum as continuous gliding voices: each component gets its own instrument (piano/violin/guitar/flute/bass/synth, by dropdown or palette preset), with the octave set by ℓ, the note by the observing band (an ascending pentatonic), and the volume by the power integrated over an octave-wide band. Each instrument has its own articulation and convolution reverb.

Things to try (Sonifier)

  • ℓ-Sweep — moves a bandpass window across multipole in time (low/bass → high/treble). Drag the position slider to scrub. Options: log volume (dB scaling), linear ℓ (vs log), ping-pong (bounce), and fixed pitch (hold the pitch at 440 Hz so you hear only amplitude/timbre change, not the confounding pitch).
  • Build-Up — progressively layers CMB → +tSZ → +CIB so you hear the total assemble from its parts.
  • ν-Sweep — crossfades across the bands so you hear the foreground transition from CMB-dominated to CIB-dominated.
  • Stereo (headphones) — Components L/R puts different components in each ear for A/B comparison; 93|353 GHz puts a CMB-dominated band in one ear and a CIB-dominated band in the other.
  • 🌟 Big Bang — a 12-second standalone piece (after Cramer 2003) that scales the actual CMB acoustic peaks (ℓ=220, 540, 810, 1120, …) to audible frequencies with their measured relative amplitudes, the pitch slowly descending to evoke cosmic expansion.
  • 3D Spatial (headphones) — places each component at a fixed direction around you (CMB ahead, tSZ left, CIB right) via HRTF panning; sphere clicks become localized drum hits at the real sky position.
  • Interactive sphere — shows the sky for the selected component/band. Click any point for a drum hit whose pitch encodes the temperature there, a texture modulated by the local fluctuation, and pings from nearby massive clusters (pitch = halo mass). Toggle Scan mode to fire all three continuously while dragging — over the cluster-dominated tSZ map it becomes a field of discrete chirps against near-silence.

Reproducibility

Spectra are precomputed from the simulation alm files; the web app synthesizes all audio client-side with the Web Audio API and renders with Canvas 2D — no server, no JavaScript dependencies. Sky Explorer images are high-resolution equirectangular renders. See the accompanying writeup for the full method.
